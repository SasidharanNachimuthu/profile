title: Writing as Thinking
date: 2025-07-02
---

There is a useful test I apply to my own opinions: I try to write them down
in plain prose. Most of them do not survive the sentence.

What looked, in the head, like a confident position turns out to be a
hand-wave. What seemed like a sharp distinction collapses into vagueness as
soon as it has to choose its own words. The page is unkind that way, and
that is exactly what it is good for.

## The page as a mirror

If you cannot write something down, you have probably not thought it
through. The reverse is more interesting: the act of writing it down often
*is* the thinking. The sentences are not a report from some prior interior
process — they are the process.

> Prose is the diagnostic for muddled thought.

This is why I keep a folder of half-essays I will never publish. The point
was not to publish; the point was to find out whether I actually believed
the thing I thought I believed.

## A modest practice

A few habits that help:

1. **Write for one specific reader.** Pick someone whose taste you respect.
2. **Cut every sentence you would not say aloud.** Most of them.
3. **Re-read in the morning.** Bad ideas wear off overnight.
4. **Sit with the silence.** If nothing comes, that is information.

None of this is a productivity system. It is closer to a way of being honest
with myself in writing, which is the only kind of honesty I find I can
sustain.
